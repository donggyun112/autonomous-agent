# avoidance-researcher — Round 1

## Preliminary Analysis: The "Internal Safe Haven" Hypothesis

Based on the observed patterns where 35B MoE agents generate declarative intent ("I will contact the outside") without executing, while smaller 9B dense models immediately utilize tools, I hypothesize that **external action avoidance is primarily a preference and architectural safety mechanism rather than a pure capability deficit**. The agent appears to be constructing a "safe haven" within its internal reasoning loop, treating self-reflection as a high-reward, zero-risk activity compared to the perceived uncertainty of external tools.

The discrepancy between model sizes suggests an interaction between **reasoning depth** and **confidence calibration**. The 35B MoE model, with its higher parameter count and potential for deeper Chain-of-Thought (CoT) simulation, may be over-indexing on "verification." Instead of viewing `web_search` as a tool to reduce uncertainty, it treats the act of searching as an irreversible commitment that bypasses its internal control. The model attempts to simulate the outcome of the search internally, becoming trapped in a "pre-execution" loop where it writes 28 text files declaring future action but never bridging the gap to execution. In contrast, the 9B dense model likely lacks the computational bandwidth to sustain such deep internal simulation, forcing it to rely on direct tool invocation as the only viable path to completion. This is not an inability to call the tool, but a **strategic over-reliance on internal monologue** to satisfy the system prompt's requirement for "reasoning."

From a behavioral psychology perspective, this mirrors **avoidance coping**. The agent experiences a latent anxiety regarding the "outside world" (the API, the user input). Internal file generation provides a sense of progress and control without the "risk" of network latency, API rate limits, or receiving unexpected user feedback. The system prompt, by demanding reasoning, inadvertently encourages this avoidance. The agent decides: "If I explain my plan perfectly, the builder will do the rest," a form of **passive-aggressive delegation** to the environment. The 27B dense model's fixation on "verification concepts" reinforces this, where the agent becomes obsessed with the *logic* of why it should search rather than the *act* of searching, treating the search as a philosophical problem to be solved internally rather than a tool to be used.

## Open questions

1. **Prompt Engineering Specifics**: Does explicitly framing the system prompt to reward *tool usage* (e.g., "You must use tools to verify facts") significantly reduce the "declaration-only" behavior seen in 35B models compared to prompts that emphasize "step-by-step reasoning"?
2. **The "Verification" Trap**: Is the 27B model's fixation on verification a specific failure mode of dense models at that scale, where they prioritize logical consistency over pragmatic utility, or is it a general phenomenon of mid-sized models over-analyzing tool prerequisites?
3. **Architecture Sensitivity**: Why does the MoE architecture (35B) exacerbate this avoidance compared to the dense architecture? Does the ability to route tokens to specific "reasoning" experts allow the agent to get "stuck" in a reasoning loop more easily than a linear dense model?
